Lithium Dosing — Always Once Daily
Formulations
Immediate release and extended release (Lithobid) have mild GI differences — some mild controversy exists on which is preferred. No need to think about it unless the patient develops nausea or diarrhea.
If nausea or diarrhea develops — switch to extended release, take with food, or lower dose if clinically able.
Dosing frequency
  • Once daily — standard for outpatient prescribing
  • Given at bedtime — enables a clean 12-hour level the next morning
  • Twice daily only for acute mania loading or very young children — outside primary care scope
Migraine caution: some data suggest lithium may paradoxically worsen both migraines and mood response in certain patients. Start very low, monitor both headache and mood. Stay at OTC or very low prescription doses until no clear harm signal. Shared decision-making throughout.
Starting and titrating
There is no minimum effective dose. (Ghaemi) — meaningful clinical signal has been observed even at very low doses including OTC lithium orotate. Start low, observe, titrate only if needed.
Start: 150mg immediate release at bedtime
Observe: signal may appear in weeks, months, or longer — allow adequate time before concluding no response
2 months: reasonable minimum observation window — can move sooner if clinical picture warrants
If not responding: titrate toward 0.6–0.75 mEq/L — supported by trial and observational data. Higher levels on the table if patient is curious and engaged.
At 150–300mg: serum level not routinely required. TSH and creatinine sufficient. Very low toxicity risk even with dehydration or NSAID exposure at this dose range.
Monitoring cadence: TSH and creatinine at 3 months, then every 6 months if stable.
After dose change (approaching 0.4+ mEq/L) or medication addition: check level after 5 days minimum.
Clinical effect may not start to be apparent until approximately 10 days after a dose change — including dose reductions.
Elderly and frail: start lower, check levels earlier even at sub-therapeutic doses.
