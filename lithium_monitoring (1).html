Monitoring lithium — what to watch and when

Establish the plan at prescribing · insulin and warfarin have sick day rules · so does lithium
Call now
Vomiting
Diarrhea
Confusion / cognitive change
Difficulty walking / ataxia
Coarse tremor — distinct from fine therapeutic tremor
Send message
Polyuria — including inability to sleep through the night · Polydipsia >2.5L/day · These are early collecting duct signals, not nuisance side effects
Thyroid — TSH
  • Baseline before starting
  • 3 months first recheck
  • q6 months if stable
  • With symptoms do not wait
  • Target TSH <2 — push toward 1 if mood symptoms persist. Do not apply general population threshold.
Renal + Calcium
  • Baseline creatinine before starting
  • q6–12 months creatinine ongoing
  • q6–12 months calcium
  • If calcium elevated reflex PTH
  • If both elevated → refer endocrine
  • EMUO early morning urine osmolality if polyuria — <300 is a collecting duct signal
  • 24FIR 24-hour fluid intake recall if polyuria — >2.5L is a collecting duct signal
Lithium levels
  • 150–300mg: level not routinely required — TSH and creatinine sufficient
  • Elderly / frail: check level at any dose
  • >300mg: check after 5 days minimum post dose change
  • Trigger meds: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, NSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors — same 5-day rule after adding or changing
  • Stable: q6 months
  • Clinical effect may lag 10 days after dose change — including reductions
Baseline documentation
  • TSH, creatinine, calcium before starting
  • Tremor character and severity — your only reference point later
  • Psoriasis history — ask before starting
  • Migraine history — discuss before starting; start low, monitor carefully
Sick day rules — in place at prescribing
  • Rehydrate with electrolytes not free water — Gatorade, Pedialyte, broth
  • If unable to keep fluids down — hold or reduce dose
  • Resume normal dose when eating and drinking normally
  • Free water dilutes sodium → drives lithium reabsorption higher
Monitoring cadence adapted from Meyer & Stahl, The Lithium Handbook. TSH targets: do not apply general population threshold (5/7/10) to mood disorder patients on lithium — subclinical hypothyroidism contributes to treatment resistance. EMUO = early morning urine osmolality; <300 mOsm/kg indicates collecting duct dysfunction — a signal for amiloride consideration, not watchful waiting. Trigger medications for level checks: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, NSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors raise lithium levels by reducing renal clearance; aspirin is safe.
